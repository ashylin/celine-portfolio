PUPILLOMETRY

Healthcare is limited

At the Digihealth Lab under Edward Wang, I designed an app for Far Red, a racially fair measurement technology used in pupillometry assessments. This innovative app leverages standard smartphone cameras, making it a valuable tool for neurological screenings.

PITFALLS

Differences really are hard to notice

Current pupillometry techniques often struggle with darker irises, where differentiating the pupil can be difficult. This raises concerns about racial bias in assessments. To address this limitation and improve overall accuracy, exploring alternative methods like Far Red technology or incorporating standardized lighting conditions during measurements are promising avenues for research and development.

PUPIL DETECTED

Novel method discovered

Our lab's new RGB camera method, spearheaded by Colin Barry and Edward Wang, expands pupillometry beyond clinical settings. It holds promise for detecting head injuries, enabling at-home cognitive screening, and even opioid testing.

EXPANSION

Diagnostic screening at home

Translating research into action, I designed a user-friendly app to guide anyone in building their own pupillometry device at home. Accessibility was paramount, ensuring users with varying technical skills could follow the instructions.

ACCESSIBILITY

Targeting Alzheimer's detection, this app prioritizes user-friendliness for older adults (age 60+). Large text ensures readability, while conveniently placed buttons minimize hand strain. Multiple learning modalities (text, images, videos) cater to individual preferences.

FINDINGS

Unmasking health disparities' hidden complexities requires relentless curiosity and problem-solving. At the Design Lab, this drive to keep digging, to never stop thinking, has become ingrained in my design process. It's fueled a deep love for the field, one that compels me to continue designing.
